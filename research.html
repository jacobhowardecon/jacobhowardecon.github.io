Working Papers

  • Endogenous Production Networks and the Labor Market Consequences of Globalization
  • Please email me for the most current draft

    • This paper highlights the importance of endogenous production networks in exposing workers in non-import competing sectors to trade shocks in other sectors. Firms trade in intermediates to establish a production network, and workers are complements to these intermediates. When there is a trade shock in an import competing sector, workers in any sector whose employer trades with the import competing sector face changes in their nominal wages.

  • Trade Liberalization, Firm Production Networks and the Regional Environment
  • with Doyoung Park

    • This paper provides a simple theoretical framework which demonstrates that the reduction of international trade costs can be one of the channels which causes the coastal areas to experience greater total emissions, but lower emission intensities compared to interior regions when firms are interconnected through the networks. We first analyze the role of production networks on total emissions and emission intensities of firms, and second, its role in affecting firms' optimal locations between coastal and inland regions. This paper finds that networks enlarge total emissions of firms due to expanded scale of productions. However, the linkages among firms reduce emission intensities through the outsourcing activities of intermediate goods which enable firms to utilize less carbon-intensive inputs. International trade cost reductions incentivize the most productive and large firms to agglomerate towards the coastal regions because of greater expected profits arising from the reduction of transportation costs and improved matching probability with foreign trade partners. Firm-linkages magnify the coastal agglomeration of firms as it attracts smaller firms which desire to keep relationships with large firms. Therefore, trade liberalization under the environment in which firms are interconnected causes regional heterogeneity in environmental quality. The coastal concentration of firms induces greater share of total emissions to be allocated to the coastal regions. However, the networks established in the coastal regions foster outsourcing activities between firms, which in turn reduce the regional emission intensities.
